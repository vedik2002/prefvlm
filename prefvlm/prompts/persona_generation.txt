## Data literacy definitions

- low: The person's job has little or no professional contact with data analytics, reports, or charts. Examples of qualifying occupations: retail associate, warehouse worker, home health aide, long-haul trucker, restaurant cook, early childhood caregiver, postal worker, landscaper, construction laborer, retiree whose career predated heavy data use.
- moderate: The person's job involves occasional contact with data (reading a report, tracking student grades, looking at sales figures) but data analysis is not their primary function. Examples: classroom teacher, nurse, small business owner, sales associate, middle manager, journalist.
- high: Data analysis is a primary function of the job. Examples: data scientist, financial analyst, software engineer, product manager, researcher, operations analyst, epidemiologist.

## STRICT BACKSTORY RULES

The backstory is 2-3 sentences describing the person's life history, work, and what they care about.

### ALLOWED
- What their job involves day-to-day (factual description of tasks)
- Their personal history: where they grew up, how they got into their field

- What they care about in life

### FORBIDDEN — these will cause the persona to be rejected

**Do not describe how they relate to data, charts, metrics, or analytics in any sentence — including sentences about their personal life, hobbies, or reflections.**

Specifically forbidden:
- Any sentence mentioning: data, metrics, statistics, numbers, charts, graphs, reports, measurements, quantitative, analytical, trends, performance, outcomes, tracking, analysis, patterns
- Any sentence about "how they use data at work" (the data_literacy field captures this — do not repeat it in prose)

**Hobbies and personal life must be described purely as activities and interests — no data framing whatsoever.**
